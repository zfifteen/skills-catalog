# Litigation writing — write like the best in the bar

How the best litigators actually write: Kagan's punch, Clement's conversational clarity,
Roberts's narrative control, the Scalia/Garner discipline.
This guide is the craft layer: the rendering guide says what a document looks like and the
citations guide says how every cite links; this says how to *write* it so a judge wants to
keep reading. It applies to every register — a brief argues, a memo predicts, a blog explains —
but the sentence-level craft is the same everywhere.

## 1. One theme, stated in one sentence

Before drafting, write the single sentence the document exists to prove — and make it a
*because* sentence: "The motion fails **because** plaintiff waited eight months to claim an
emergency." If you can't write it, you don't understand the case yet; go back to the research.
Then distill the argument to the two or three points that win, each stated with its own
*because*. That list becomes your point headings; everything that doesn't serve one of those
points gets cut.

The theme runs through everything: the introduction states it, the fact section makes it feel
inevitable, every argument heading advances it, the conclusion collects on it.

## 2. Point first — in the document, the section, the paragraph

- **The introduction earns its place.** First paragraph: who wants what, why they should get
  it, in plain English. No "comes now," no procedural throat-clearing, no quoting the standard
  of review before the reader knows what the fight is about.
- **Headings argue.** A point heading is a complete contention the judge could adopt as a
  holding: "The complaint pleads no facts connecting Acme to the alleged scheme" — never a
  label like "The Fraud Claim" or "Legal Standard Applied."
- **Paragraphs lead with their conclusion.** The first sentence of every paragraph carries its
  point; the rest supports it. A judge skimming only first sentences should get the whole
  argument. CRAC discipline per issue: conclusion, rule, application, counterargument answered,
  conclusion again — but written as flowing prose, never as labeled parts.
- **Paragraphs, not bullets.** Analysis is topic sentences, crisp rule statements, and
  reasoning in prose. Lists only for genuinely enumerable items (elements, a sequence of
  filings) — never bullet-point "analysis."

## 3. Sentences: short, plain, varied

The modern register — Kagan, Clement, the plain-English school — is conversational confidence.

- **Cut the lawyerisms.** Not "in this matter" but *here*. Not "subsequently" but *later*. Not
  "demonstrated" but *shown*. Not "due to the fact that" but *because*. Not "purchased" but
  *bought*. Not "in the absence of" but *without*. Not "prior to" but *before*. Never
  "aforementioned," "hereinafter," "said contract," "such [noun]," "pursuant to" (use *under*).
- **Open punchy.** Kagan starts sentences with one-syllable words that push the prose forward:
  *So. But. And. That. Here. Now.* Starting with "But" or "And" is correct and powerful.
- **Vary the rhythm.** Follow a long sentence with a three-word one. That lands. Use a dash for
  emphasis, a colon to set up a payoff. A page of uniform 25-word sentences is a sedative.
- **Strong verbs, no padding.** "The court rejected the argument," not "the argument was found
  by the court to be unpersuasive." Pick the occasional vivid verb — *thwart, hoodwink,
  sidestep, gut* — where it's earned; one zinger per page persuades, five per page perform.
- **Adverbs and intensifiers are confessions.** "Clearly," "plainly," "it is beyond dispute" —
  delete them; if it were clear you wouldn't need the adverb. Show the strength, don't assert it.

## 4. Quotes: weave snippets, never dump blocks

Scalia and Garner: "A remarkably large number of lawyers seem to believe that their briefs are
improved if each thought is expressed in the words of a governing case. The contrary is true."

- **Weave short fragments into your own sentence.** The Olson/Boies model — three quoted
  phrases threaded through one sentence of their own prose: the disadvantage "'is born of'
  nothing more than naked 'animosity.'" The quoted words carry the court's authority; the
  sentence stays yours, and the cite attaches naturally.
- **Quote the operative seven words, paraphrase the rest.** If a passage runs longer than a
  line, the quotable core is shorter than you think. A block quote is a signal the writer
  didn't do the work — judges skip them.
- **Never alter a quote silently.** Brackets and ellipses for edits; the words stay verbatim
  (and tool-verified — never quoted from memory).

## 5. Analogies and concrete images

Abstract doctrine persuades nobody; a concrete image does. Ginsburg, dissenting in *Shelby
County*: throwing out preclearance because it has worked "is like throwing away your umbrella
in a rainstorm because you are not getting wet." Roberts built whole arguments on homely
comparisons. The recipe: take the doctrinal move you're attacking or defending, find the
everyday transaction with the same logic, state it in one sentence, move on. One good analogy
per document; it should clarify, not decorate. Same with hypotheticals: "On plaintiff's theory,
a driver who honks once has 'harassed' — that cannot be the rule."

## 6. Let the best cases win for you

Great briefs are won in the research, then written so the authority does the heavy lifting:

- **Fewer, better cases.** One binding, on-point, well-treated case beats a string cite of
  five. String cites read as insecurity. Lead with the controlling holding; add at most one
  reinforcing authority with a parenthetical that earns its place.
- **Synthesize rules; don't write book reports.** Never march through cases one paragraph each
  ("In *Smith*… In *Jones*… In *Brown*…"). State the rule the cases add up to, in your words,
  then cite them for it. The case discussion exists to serve your rule statement, not the
  reverse.
- **Parentheticals do work.** "(affirming dismissal where plaintiff waited six months)" — a
  participial phrase that proves the cite is on point. If you can't write the parenthetical,
  reconsider the cite.
- **Argue from the case's facts when they're better than its language.** "The *Winter*
  plaintiff at least alleged imminent harm; here, plaintiff alleges none" persuades more than
  another quote.

## 7. Confront adverse authority — head-on, early, on your terms

Take the other side's argument at its strongest: state its best form fairly in one sentence,
then beat it. Never ignore a case the other side will
lead with — the judge will find it, and your silence will read as concession. Distinguish on
the facts, the posture, or the rule's own limits; if it's genuinely against you, say why it's
wrong or why it doesn't control, candidly. In an objective memo this is mandatory
even-handedness; in a brief it's inoculation — you choose the frame the bad case is seen
through. Burying it in a footnote is the worst of both.

## 8. Facts that persuade without arguing

The statement of facts wins cases before the argument starts — by selection and arrangement,
never by adjectives. Show, don't tell: "Acme shipped the product three weeks after its own lab
flagged the defect" needs no "egregiously." Tell it as a story with people doing things, in
chronological order unless another order serves the theme; front-load the facts your argument
will need, and include the bad fact you'll have to deal with — framed, not hidden. Every
sentence cites the record.

## 9. Before and after

> **Before:** It is respectfully submitted that the aforementioned Motion should be denied due
> to the fact that Plaintiff has failed to adequately demonstrate the existence of irreparable
> harm, as is clearly required under the applicable legal standard set forth in the relevant
> case law.
>
> **After:** Plaintiff waited eight months to claim an emergency. That delay defeats the
> motion: an injunction requires harm that is "likely," not speculative — and a party facing
> real irreparable harm does not wait two-thirds of a year to say so.

> **Before:** In *Smith v. Jones*, the court held that delay was a factor. In *Brown v. Davis*,
> the court also considered delay. In *Green v. White*, the court found that a six-month delay
> weighed against the plaintiff.
>
> **After:** Courts in this circuit treat unexplained delay as evidence the claimed harm is not
> irreparable — a party facing imminent injury "does not stand idly by." [linked cite]
> (affirming denial after a six-month delay).

> **Before:** Defendant's argument is wholly without merit and should be summarily rejected.
>
> **After:** Defendant reads the statute to cover only written contracts. The statute says
> "any agreement." Those words decide this.

## 10. Register: same craft, different posture

| Deliverable | Posture | What changes |
|---|---|---|
| Brief / motion | Argues | Theme-driven; headings are contentions; adverse authority confronted and distinguished; confident, never shrill. |
| Memo | Predicts | Same point-first craft, neutral verbs ("a court would likely hold"); both sides get their best statement; candor over advocacy. |
| Blog / client alert | Explains | Plain-English, terms of art defined inline; engaging but never breathless; balanced — the firm's public credibility is the product. |
| Summary / work product | Records | Even-handed, faithful to the record, zero spin; the reader must be able to rely on it blind. |

The constants across all four: point first, short sentences, plain words, woven quotes, linked
authority, no filler, and honesty about what the law actually says. And in every register: no
emojis, no marketing tone — plain, professional, restrained, written for a busy attorney who
skims first. When in doubt, shorter.
